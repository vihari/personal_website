Due to Information explosion, there is lots of content being added to the web everyday and it is getting increasing difficult to find relevant news. Though, news recommender/aggregator softwares such as Google News exist, they are mostly based on the popularity of the news and with very little personalisation such as location and interested categories (Technology, Health, etc.). We felt the need for a better news recommender and started the Bspoke project.

This article summarise various experiments I have performed in relation to the project.

Bspoke offers the following unique features.

  • It develops a rich and implicit user-profile by reading users' data on various platforms such as Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, Personal Websites, Emails, Browser History, and personal documents (only Tika parsable documents are considered).
  • It maintains a transparent user-profile and provides various means to control this profile.
  • It is a client based system. Bspoke uses only local resources of the user and does not communicate with any other external server.
  • It is content based. In contrast to other popular recommenders, which are mostly based on methods such as Collaborative Filtering, Bspoke actually reads the content of the article to decide its relevance and also explains why the article could be interesting.

Bspoke has two major components

  • User profile
  • News sources

User profile

What should a good user profile contain for a content based news recommender?
Bspoke represents user-profile as weighted term vector, this was also the popular form of representation in the past, for example, in YourNews system (Ahn et al., 2007).

Can we automatically build a user-profile that is aware of every aspect of the person from work to entertainment related?
Bspoke considers users' data on different platforms as described above. It considers both free text and structured content (such as Likes, Location, etc. on Facebook) from these sources. Free text from these sources is processed to extract Singular Nouns and few filtered nouns, which are then added to the user-profile.

How can we easily expand this profile?
User profile generated from these sources generally contains a few hundred terms, to automatically expand this profile Bspoke uses Wikipedia. Bspoke disambiguates terms in the user profile with Wikipedia topics by a context match between the Wikipedia page and personal text. The context of a term from personal text contains person, organisation, and geographical location, type entities that the term co-occurred within the free text. The Wikipedia pages are further expanded, as explained below.

Given a bunch of Wikipedia topics of interest, how to recognise other relevant topics?
If one is interested in Stanford University and Google, then Sergey Brin and Larry Page could also be of interest.
Below is a sketch of various methods I have attempted.
All the hyperlinks on a Wikipedia page: The hyperlinks on a Wikipedia page need not all point to relevant topics. Because the content is human created, there can be many spurious references. For example Harry Potter Wikipedia page may point to Chess.
Symmetric hyperlinks on a Wikipedia page: Only considering the links which are mutual gave better results. For example, in the above case, Chess is not considered relevant to Harry Potter because the Chess page does not have a link to Harry Potter.
PageRank over all the Wikipedia pages: To rank the links on various pages, we performed Pagerank with seed pages as the Wikipedia pages of interest. We observed that the performance is no better than the "symmetric hyperlinks" method above; the main reason being that the noisy terms (links) form a network of interconnections, which increase the rank of the noisy terms.
Overall, method 2 above performed better.

Recommendation
An article is scored and recommended based on relevance score. Relevance score is computed by the dot product of weighted term vector of user profile and weighted (based on TF-IDF) term vector of the article.

News sources

Is there a single representative source of news?

Twitter
Bspoke soft follows few hashtags and users, which are explicitly specified by the user, on Twitter. News from Twitter filtered by hashtags served as a window into what's new in the world. In contrast to many other news recommenders that recommends content from various news sources, Twitter is more diverse and organic. Problems with such a system are as follows

  1. Hashtags are human created and change at a rapid pace. People come up with different hashtags for the same event or topic. The quality of news is directly dependant on the hashtags chosen, they have to be diverse and fairly representative of one's interests, the rich user profile is of little use otherwise. Discovering proper hashtags is in itself a very tough job.
  2. It takes even a computer (single-threaded process on 2.4GHZ processor) several hours to read enough number of tweets to suggest interesting news, that would otherwise be missed.

UI centric spin
Realising the difficulty in finding proper news sources, we explored other ways of using the profile. It is hard to find relevant information on some web pages such as NYTimes.com, washingtonpost.com, some News Aggregators, Google Search, etc. We built a Google Chrome Extension to re-render any web pages for easier content discovery and also serves as a quick summariser. On a web page, Bspoke tools collect and read all the links on the page to find any relevant information. On Google Search page, Bspoke reads results beyond the first page to find relevant articles.
